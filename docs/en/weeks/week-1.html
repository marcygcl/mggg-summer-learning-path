Foundations

3 min read · Status: Done

Where I was starting from

First week. I'm coming from geospatial and census work, so the data isn't foreign — but electoral analysis is. My goal here was simply to understand how this kind of analysis is even done, before touching anything about Kansas City.

What I explored

The census fundamentals. VAP — voting age population — counts everyone 18 and older, citizens and non-citizens alike, and comes straight from the decennial PL 94-171 file (the P3 table). CVAP — citizen voting age population — narrows that to citizens, and isn't published directly at small geographies; it's estimated from the ACS. Both matter: VAP for context, CVAP for who can actually vote.

Then the demographic categories and the ordering convention — Black → Hispanic → Asian → AMIN → Other → White — that assigns each person once and prevents double-counting. And the shorthand that follows from it: BVAP, HVAP, AVAP, WVAP.

The moment it clicked

Aha

The category order is a deliberate methodological choice, not bookkeeping. Change the order and you change the counts — and therefore the analysis. The data is never neutral; a convention is already an argument.
